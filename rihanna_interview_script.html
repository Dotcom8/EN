Interview script — Rihanna at the Caribbean Festival

Podcast format · Pair work · Target: 5+ minutes speaking time

Achievements ✓ Difficult choices ✓ UK / host country ✓ Language & culture (Patois) ✓ Future of Barbados ✓ Biography ✓
Interviewer
Welcome, Rihanna! Can you introduce yourself to our audience?

Your bullet points:

  • Welcome her warmly
  • Ask: name, where she's from, how she'd describe herself
Rihanna

Thank you so much for having me — this is exactly the kind of celebration I love! So, my full name is Robyn Rihanna Fenty, and I was born on February 20th, 1988, in Saint Michael, Barbados. A tiny island, big personality — that's us! I grew up in a small house in Bridgetown, the capital. My mum is part Irish and part Barbadian, my dad is Barbadian, and I have two younger brothers. Music was always around me, but honestly, I was just a girl from Bajan who loved to sing in the street. I never imagined any of this.

Interviewer
How did your music career begin? Biography

Your bullet points:

  • Ask: when did she start singing?
  • Ask: how was she discovered?
  • Ask: first big moment?
Rihanna

It's a bit of a fairy tale, honestly. When I was about sixteen, an American music producer called Evan Rogers came to Barbados on holiday. A friend of mine knew him and arranged an audition — just me singing in a hotel room. He was so impressed that he brought me to New York to record some demo tracks. And then — this is the part that still gives me chills — Jay-Z himself heard my demo and signed me to his record label, Def Jam Recordings, in 2005. I had to leave Barbados at just sixteen years old. My first album, Music of the Sun, came out that same year. It was all incredibly fast. My mum cried when I left, and so did I — but I knew I had to go. You don't say no to that kind of opportunity.

Interviewer
Your song "Work" uses a lot of Patois. Why was that important to you? Language & culture

Your bullet points:

  • Ask about the song "Work"
  • Ask: what is Patois / Bajan Creole?
  • Ask: why use it in a pop song?
Rihanna

Oh, "Work" — that song is me, completely. So, Patois, also written as patwa, is a Creole language spoken across the Caribbean — it mixes West African languages with English, French, and Spanish from the colonial period. In Barbados we call ours Bajan Creole. It's what we speak at home, with family, in the market. For a long time, people in the industry told me: "tone it down, be more neutral, don't confuse the audience." And I did, for many years. But "Work" was me saying — no. This is how we actually speak. This is how we actually sound. The lyrics are dense and rhythmic exactly because Patois is dense and rhythmic. When the song came out, millions of Caribbean people heard their own voice in a global number-one hit for the first time. That matters more to me than any chart position.

Interviewer
You became much more than a musician. Can you tell us about Fenty Beauty? Achievements

Your bullet points:

  • Ask about Fenty Beauty — what is it?
  • Ask: why was it different from other makeup brands?
  • Ask: she became a billionaire — how does that feel?
Rihanna

So I launched Fenty Beauty in 2017, and the thing that made it different was very simple: I released 40 shades of foundation on day one — from the palest skin to the deepest, darkest tones. That had never been done before at that scale by a major beauty brand. Women of colour had always been an afterthought. I said — you are not an afterthought. You are the whole point. The launch completely changed how the beauty industry works, to the point where they now call this change the "Fenty Effect." And yes, it made me a billionaire — the first Barbadian billionaire, in fact. But honestly? The moment that meant more was seeing a Black woman in Lagos, or Kingston, or Bridgetown, finding her exact shade for the first time. That's the achievement. I also have Savage X Fenty, my lingerie brand, which uses the same philosophy — every body, every size, celebrated.

Interviewer
In 2021, Barbados became a republic AND named you a National Hero. What did that moment mean? Nation of origin

Your bullet points:

  • Ask about Barbados becoming a republic in 2021
  • Ask: what does "National Hero" mean to her?
  • Ask: how did she feel being named alongside the ceremony?
Rihanna

November 30th, 2021 — I will never forget that night. Barbados officially removed the British monarch as head of state and became a republic after 55 years of independence. And right there, at the ceremony, Prime Minister Mia Mottley made me the eleventh National Hero of Barbados. I was standing in my homeland, in the dark, under all those stars, and I just — I could not hold back the tears. You have to understand what that means for a girl from Bridgetown. National heroes are people like Errol Barrow, the father of our independence. To be placed in that company is overwhelming. It also felt like a statement about what Caribbean excellence looks like today — it's not just cricket and politics. It's also a girl who sang her way out of the island and then spent twenty years making sure the world remembered exactly where she came from.

Interviewer
What have been the most difficult choices in your career? Difficult choices

Your bullet points:

  • Ask: did she ever want to give up?
  • Ask about difficult personal moments
  • Ask: choosing between music and business?
Rihanna

There are two I think about the most. The first was leaving Barbados at sixteen. That sounds exciting, but it's also terrifying. You leave your mum, your school, your language, your food, your identity — you arrive in New York and you are suddenly very foreign, very small. The industry wanted me to change my accent, change my style, become something more palatable. That pressure never fully goes away, and resisting it is a constant choice. The second was in 2009. I was in a very public and very painful situation — domestic violence — and the whole world was watching. I had to choose between retreating and disappearing, or showing up. I showed up. I released Rated R in 2009 — a darker, harder album — and refused to be defined by what happened to me. That took every bit of strength I had. And I think it changed what people expected from me, and frankly what I expected from myself.

Interviewer
What is your relationship with the United Kingdom? UK / host country

Your bullet points:

  • Ask: does she spend time in the UK?
  • Ask: UK fans / UK music scene?
  • Ask: Barbados and the UK — colonial history?
Rihanna

The UK has always been incredibly important to me — my music was embraced there very early, and British music culture has deeply influenced Caribbean music for decades. You can't talk about reggae, ska, or even modern grime without talking about the Windrush generation — Caribbean people who came to Britain from the 1940s onwards, who built communities, who kept their culture alive under enormous pressure. That story is our story too. Now, the relationship between Barbados and Britain is also a complicated one. Barbados was a British colony for over 300 years. The sugar plantations, the enslaved people, the entire colonial economy — that is the foundation. Barbados becoming a republic in 2021 was a peaceful but very deliberate way of saying: we honour our own history, we govern ourselves now. I think that's healthy. You can have a relationship with Britain and still insist on your own identity. That's what I try to do personally, and what Barbados did politically.

Interviewer
What is your message to young people from the Caribbean today? Cultural future

Your bullet points:

  • Ask: what advice would she give?
  • Ask: is it important to stay connected to your roots?
Rihanna

Never, ever let anyone make you feel like your accent is a problem, or your culture is less sophisticated, or that you need to sand down your edges to succeed in the world. I spent years being told to do that, and the moment I stopped — the moment I leaned in to being Bajan, to using Patois, to celebrating where I came from — that is when I found the biggest success. Your roots are not a weight. They are a superpower. I also want young Caribbean women especially to know that the world is yours. You don't have to choose between being ambitious and being rooted. You can build a billion-dollar company and still know every word to a soca song at Crop Over festival. Both things are true. Both things matter.

Interviewer
Finally — what do you see as the future of Barbados and the Caribbean? Future — political, environmental, cultural

Your bullet points:

  • Ask about: climate change and the sea
  • Ask about: political independence / republic status
  • Ask about: the cultural future — music, art, identity
Rihanna

Three things worry me and three things give me hope. The worry: climate change is an existential threat to the Caribbean. We are talking about islands. If sea levels rise by one metre, we lose coastlines, we lose communities, we lose homes. The Caribbean produces less than one percent of global carbon emissions, but we are among the first to suffer the consequences. That is a profound injustice, and I use my platform to say so as loudly as I can. Second, the region needs to deepen its own economic independence — not just from Britain, but from all foreign dependency. And third, we need to protect our creative culture, because it is constantly being borrowed and monetised by others without credit. But here is the hope: Barbados's republican moment showed that political self-determination is possible and peaceful. Caribbean music — from dancehall to soca to Afrobeats hybrids — is dominating global charts right now. Young Caribbean people are prouder of who they are than any generation before them. I genuinely believe the best chapter of this story is still ahead.

Interviewer
Rihanna, thank you so much. One last question — why did you agree to come to this Caribbean Festival today?

Your bullet points:

  • Ask: why this festival?
  • Thank her warmly at the end
Rihanna

Because this is exactly the kind of space that should exist. A place where Caribbean heroes — whether they are poets, athletes, politicians, or musicians — are celebrated, studied, and heard. Too often, the Caribbean is seen as a holiday destination, a backdrop, a postcard. This festival says: no, there are real stories here, real struggles, real triumphs. That is something I will always show up for. And honestly? Any excuse to talk about Barbados — I'll take it. Big up the island! Thank you.
